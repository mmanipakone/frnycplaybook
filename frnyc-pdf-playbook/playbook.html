01

Introduction & How to Use This Playbook

Who this is for, how to navigate it, and the key distinctions between Year 0 and Year 1+ schools.

Who This Playbook Is For

The FRNYC Recruitment & Retention Playbook is for everyone responsible for building and sustaining a FRNYC cohort in a NYC public high school — program coordinators, school counselors, APs of instruction, and staff at new partner schools preparing to launch FRNYC for the first time.

This playbook addresses two distinct audiences with different starting points:

Year 0 — New FRNYC Schools
  • Setting up FRNYC from scratch
  • Designating your FRNYC coordinator before November
  • Building counselor buy-in before winter break
  • Communicating pathway codes in NYCDOE HS application
  • Planning your first open house events
  • Setting realistic baseline enrollment targets with OSP
Year 1+ — Existing FRNYC Schools
  • Reviewing last year's data and conversion rates
  • Rebuilding the ambassador corps from completers
  • Improving interest survey design and response rate
  • Closing specific equity gaps (SWD, MLL representation)
  • Strengthening the summer gap retention touchpoint
  • Aligning pathway info design to student relevance framework

How to Read This

Read the full playbook once before your recruitment season begins. Then use the Quick Reference Cards (Appendix B) as your week-by-week operating guide. Share the Messaging Guide (Appendix A) with anyone who speaks about FRNYC — from school staff to student ambassadors.

⚡

This playbook is prescriptive, not aspirational. Every section answers three questions: what to do, why it works, and when to do it. If a recommendation can't answer all three, it has not earned its place in this document.

Key Definitions

FRNYC / FutureReady NYC The NYC DOE program placing high school students into career pathway courses, work-based learning, and early college credit. Managed by the Office of Student Pathways (OSP).
Recruitment The full system from first awareness through confirmed enrollment. It is not a communications campaign. It is a year-round relationship-building operation.
Retention Keeping students enrolled and engaged through 12th grade credential completion. Recruitment without retention wastes both the school's investment and the student's opportunity.
SWD / MLL Students with Disabilities and Multilingual Learners — frequently underrepresented in FRNYC pathways not because they're uninterested, but because structural barriers push them out.
KIMS The NYC DOE data system tracking enrollment, WBL hours logged, and ECC credits accrued. October 1 enrollment numbers are the primary accountability metric.
WBL / ECC Work-Based Learning (internships, job shadows, simulations) and Early College Credit (college-level coursework completed before graduation).

"Recruitment is not an event; it is a year-round process that begins in 8th grade, bleeding into 9th. Schools that wait until 10th grade to introduce pathways consistently struggle to fill cohorts with genuinely interested students."

02

Five Core Principles

These principles hold across every school, every grade, and every stakeholder. They explain why some FRNYC schools consistently outperform others.

Every tactic in this playbook is an application of one or more of these five principles. Before implementing any strategy, ask: which principle does this support, and is that the right one for this moment in the cycle?

01
Timing

Start before it feels urgent.

Recruitment is a year-round process that begins in 8th grade, bleeding into 9th. Schools that treat it as a spring campaign consistently miss the window. Every semester has a distinct recruitment goal. Missing one means starting the next from behind — and there is no catch-up month.

02
Integration

Make it school culture, not a side project.

FRNYC must live in school culture and curriculum — not in one coordinator's inbox. The Career Explorations course, advisory mentions, staff buy-in, and visible student success are all recruitment infrastructure. A coordinator recruiting alone will always underperform a school where FRNYC is woven into daily operations.

03
Social Proofing

Students follow students.

Students choose what students who look like them have chosen. Near-peer ambassadors, alumni panels, and visible student work are among the highest-converting recruitment investments available. A senior speaking authentically for 3 minutes at an advisory outperforms a brochure by an order of magnitude. Build your ambassador corps before November — not in March.

04
Relevance

Lead with what they get, not what FRNYC is.

Students enroll and stay when they see how a pathway connects to their real life. Abstract language about "career readiness" does not move students. Specific language does: "You could earn your EPA certification for free — that credential normally costs $30,000." Students who chose a pathway aligned to personal meaning stay. Students who drifted into one often leave.

05
Equity

Underrepresentation is a systems problem — not a preferences problem.

SWD and MLL students are frequently underrepresented in FRNYC pathways not because they aren't interested, but because structural barriers push them out before anyone notices. Strong programs actively track who is and isn't enrolled, set explicit equity targets, and problem-solve barriers before they become attrition. Equity is not a separate initiative — it is a test of whether the other four principles are actually working.

03

The Recruitment Architecture

A five-stage, year-round system — not a one-time campaign. Each stage requires specific actions, not just awareness.

Effective FRNYC recruitment follows a five-stage architecture. The stages are sequential — skipping or compressing any stage reduces conversion at the next. Awareness without exploration is just information. Exploration without commitment is just curiosity. This sequence is not aspirational; it is the operating logic of every high-performing FRNYC school.

①

Awareness

Students learn FRNYC exists and that it's for students like them. Channel: announcements, Career Explorations course, peer word-of-mouth, 8th grade open houses.

Risk: Curiosity is fragile. Awareness without follow-up decays within days.

②

Exploration

Students have meaningful contact with what the pathway delivers: alumni panels, site visits, near-peer presentations, Decision Day previews.

Goal: Identity integration — "this is for someone like me."

③

Commitment

Students make a formal decision. Decision Day is a ritual, not a form. Public commitments are more durable than private ones.

Risk: Summer gap erodes commitment. Check in.

④

Experience

Students are enrolled and actively participating. Quality drives retention: relevant curriculum, strong teachers, WBL placements tied to stated interests.

⑤

Completion

Students earn credentials (ECC, WBL hours, certifications) before graduation. Completers are your strongest next-year recruiters.

"Stickiness comes from coupling awareness with exposure in a structured sequence. At a point when interest is integrated into their sense of self, identity is more durable than interest."

Enrollment Funnel Benchmarks

Any conversion rate more than 10 percentage points below benchmark at the midpoint of the cycle triggers an immediate review and a specific intervention plan — not just increased promotion.

Awareness

Exploration

Applied
~54% complete interest survey or application
Enrolled

04

Filling the Pipeline — 8th Grade Recruitment

The pipeline that feeds 9th grade begins in 8th. Schools that wait lose two years of cultivation before they realize it.

The 9th grade pathway begins in 8th grade. Schools that wait until 10th grade to introduce pathways consistently struggle to fill cohorts with genuinely interested students. Building relationships with middle school counselors, running quality open houses, and activating student ambassadors before the NYCDOE application deadline are the three highest-leverage investments in the incoming pipeline.

Small-Group Touchpoints — The Highest-Converting Strategy

Bring currently enrolled FRNYC students to every fair and open house. Students who have experience in the pathway can speak to the value it will present for a prospective student and family. Those who completed an internship, earned college credits, or went on a career trip can speak concretely about what the program delivers. Formalizing an ambassador role for these students — with a short script and clear talking points — is a low-cost, high-impact recruitment investment.

Key Moment

The open house 2–3 weeks before the NYCDOE application deadline is the highest-leverage event in 8th grade recruitment. Everything else supports it. Plan backward from this date.

Signal of Interest — The NYCDOE HS Application

The NYCDOE high school application is the equivalent of a formal interest survey at this stage. Students who list an FRNYC-affiliated school's pathway code as their first or second choice have self-identified as interested. Schools should communicate specific pathway codes clearly at open houses and on all materials, and should recommend that families list both codes (first and second choice within the same school) to maximize the chance of placement. The application window typically stays open about one month; schools that want to maximize access should build an internal buffer extension (e.g., publicly the deadline is May 1, internally it stays open until May 15).

What to Do at Open Houses

  • Show what the pathway looks like through panels, site visits, and near-peer testimonials
  • Have student ambassadors give 2–3 minute talks with a clear script
  • Display actual student work — projects, portfolios, credentials earned
  • Announce the pathway code and walk families through the application together in-session
  • Collect contact info for follow-up (separate from the NYCDOE app)

After the Open House

  • Follow up within 48 hours with interested families via email or text
  • Share a brief summary of pathway outcomes (credential, wages, college credit)
  • Track which students listed your pathway code; flag for personal outreach
  • Students who said yes but can't be scheduled due to AP/IEP conflicts get an individual conversation — not a form rejection
⚡

Year 0 tip: If this is your school's first year, prioritize 2 high-quality open house events over 5 generic ones. Depth converts. Breadth builds name recognition but rarely fills seats.

05

The Internal Recruitment Cycle — 9th Grade

The most important recruitment event of the year happens inside your school, in May, with every 9th grader. Everyone responds.

Once students arrive in 9th grade, the internal recruitment cycle takes over. This cycle has four phases — Generate Awareness, Interest Survey, Review & Reconcile, Confirm & Close — each requiring specific, coordinated action. No phase is optional.

Phase 1 — Generate Awareness (September through Spring Break)

The 9th grade year begins with the Career Explorations course. Students learn about pathways and how each relates to their real life. This is a crucial point to inform students fully and honestly about what each pathway requires and what it delivers — and to get them aligned with their interests before the survey goes out.

Near-peer presentations run throughout this window. Currently enrolled FRNYC seniors — including those who did not originally choose FRNYC — visit advisories and speak authentically. Schools that run Foundations of Health lunch workshops report 75%+ opt-in from 9th graders who attended.

Phase 2 — Interest Survey (May)

In early May, a presentation is delivered in all 9th grade advisories. Within 1–2 weeks, a Google Form interest survey goes to every 9th grader.

Critical Design Choice

The survey is NOT an opt-in form. Every student must respond: Yes, I'm interested; No, I'm not interested; or Maybe, I want to know more. This ensures no one is missed and no one is assumed to be uninterested. The opt-in AND opt-out survey to all 9th graders in May is the highest-leverage action of the internal recruitment cycle.

Phase 3 — Review & Reconcile (With Leadership)

The principal, AP of Instruction, and grade team leader review survey results together. Pathway teachers who taught the 9th grade preview course weigh in on which students they believe are ready. The team cross-references results against the master schedule: AP class conflicts, mandated services, IEP accommodations, and grade repetition situations may prevent some interested students from participating. Students who said yes but can't be scheduled receive an individual conversation.

Phase 4 — Confirm & Close

Accepted students receive congratulations letters, FRNYC stickers, or other branded materials. An end-of-year survey captures any remaining student interest before summer, which is used for September backfill. The fall schedule for 10th grade is built around the confirmed cohort list.

⚡

The summer gap is an underused retention touchpoint. A postcard, a group chat message, or a short summer check-in between June acceptance and September arrival prevents enthusiasm from fading. Schools that skip this consistently see unexpected September dropouts from students who were confirmed in May.

06

Pathway Information Design

Students and families are more likely to enroll and persist when pathway information is clear, concrete, and directly connected to real outcomes.

Schools should move away from generic program descriptions and adopt a consistent, student-centered format. Abstract language about "career readiness" does not move students. Specific language does: "You could earn your EPA certification for refrigerants for free, which normally costs $30,000."

Every FRNYC pathway should be able to answer four questions. If your pathway cannot answer all four clearly and specifically, students and families will not enroll — or will not stay.

The Outcome — Why It Matters

What careers does this open?

  • Clear examples of careers and associated wages
  • Emphasis on tangible benefits: income, credentials, college credits
  • Avoid abstract terms like "career readiness" or "industry exposure"
The Skills — What Students Will Learn

What will students be able to do?

  • Specific, practical skills students will develop
  • Focus on what students can do, not what teachers will cover
  • Concrete demonstrations: portfolio, certification, project
The Experience — What Students Will Actually Do

What does the day-to-day look like?

  • Examples of projects, fieldwork, and work-based learning
  • Real-world exposure: sites, partners, environments
  • Photos of actual student work — not stock images
The Payoff — What Students Gain Before Graduation

What do they leave with?

  • Certifications earned (industry-recognized)
  • College credits completed before graduation
  • Work experience and internship documentation

Generic vs. Effective Messaging

✗ Generic — Don't Use This ✓ Effective — Use This
"Students will develop career readiness skills in a healthcare pathway." "By June, you'll have 54 hours of clinical experience at a hospital partner and an EMT certification that can get you a job starting at $19/hour."
"This technology pathway prepares students for careers in computing." "You'll build a portfolio of 3 coded projects, earn a Google IT Support certificate, and be eligible for college-level CS credit at CUNY."
⚡

Students who choose pathways aligned to personal meaning stay. Students who were placed or drifted into a pathway often leave. Build structures that help students reflect on who they are and what they care about before committing — not after enrollment opens.

07

Retention — Keeping Students Through Completion

Filling seats at the start of the year is not the goal. Filling credentials at the end of four years is.

Students who enrolled but don't complete represent a double failure: a missed credential for the student and a wasted recruitment investment for the school. Retention is a recruitment problem — the causes of dropout are almost always visible at the enrollment stage if schools know what to look for.

🎯

Pillar 1 — Relevance

  • Students stay when the pathway connects to real life and career goals — this must be established before enrollment, not after
  • Provide structured exploration opportunities (panels, site visits, job shadows) before asking students to commit
  • Interest surveys should ask "why does this pathway matter to you?" — not just "are you interested?"
  • Pathway materials must use the Outcome/Skills/Experience/Payoff format — abstract descriptions predict dropout
👩‍🏫

Pillar 2 — Teacher Quality

  • Teachers who double as advisors are the single most consequential retention factor after relevance
  • A "good fit" pathway teacher reviews curriculum and adapts it for their specific students — they don't just cover material
  • Teachers hold advising-style conversations about student identity, interests, and pathway fit
  • Students in focus groups consistently cite teacher relationships as a primary reason for staying or leaving
🧭

Pillar 3 — Advising

  • One-on-one advising is the highest-leverage retention tool — it surfaces misalignment before dropout
  • Early warning signs: dropping advisory, missing WBL hours, declining grades in pathway course
  • Intervention: individual conversation, not a group session — group sessions signal that the problem is systemic, not personal
  • Don't wait for a quarter to end. Act within one week of observing an early warning sign

The Summer Gap — An Underused Retention Touchpoint

The period between June acceptance and September arrival consistently erodes commitment. Students get summer jobs, lose track of their plans, or simply forget the excitement of Decision Day. A postcard, a group chat message, or a 5-minute phone call prevents enthusiasm from fading. This is the lowest-cost, highest-leverage retention investment available.

Early Warning Signs (Act Within 1 Week)

  • Student misses 3+ advisory sessions in a row
  • WBL hours significantly below cohort average at midpoint
  • Student directly expresses disinterest in pathway content
  • Grade in pathway course drops below 65

What Doesn't Work for Retention

  • Waiting until the end of a quarter to check in
  • Group interventions when the problem is individual misalignment
  • Assuming disengagement means disinterest in FRNYC broadly
  • Not tracking WBL hours until the final semester
08

Equity — SWD & MLL Access

Underrepresentation is not a sign of disinterest. It is a sign of structural barriers that schools are responsible for removing.

Students with Disabilities (SWD) and Multilingual Learners (MLL) are frequently underrepresented in FRNYC pathways — not because they aren't interested, but because structural barriers (scheduling, language access, ECC prerequisites) push them out. Strong programs actively track who is and isn't enrolled, set explicit equity targets, and problem-solve barriers before they become attrition.

1
Scheduling Conflicts (SWD)

IEP mandated services, AP class placement, and grade repetition pull SWD students out of pathway time slots before anyone notices.

Fix

Principal + AP cross-reference survey results with master schedule before finalizing cohort. Students blocked by schedule get individual conversations and alternative slots — not form rejections.

2
Language Access (MLL)

Open house materials, interest surveys, and pathway descriptions are English-only. MLL families receive less information and make less-informed decisions.

Fix

Translate all core pathway materials (1-pager, interest survey, Decision Day communication) into the top 3 home languages of the school. Recruit bilingual near-peer ambassadors.

3
ECC Prerequisites (Both)

Some Early College Credit courses have prerequisite requirements that disproportionately exclude SWD and MLL students who are on track but not in accelerated sequences.

Fix

Audit prerequisite lists annually. Identify which barriers are structural requirements vs. inherited assumptions. Work with OSP to expand access pathways for qualified SWD/MLL students.

4
Lack of Representation in Outreach

Pathway materials and ambassador corps feature limited representation of SWD and MLL students, signaling that these pathways are "not for them."

Fix

Intentionally recruit SWD and MLL alumni as ambassadors. Review all visual materials for representation. Partner with ENL and Special Education departments for co-outreach.

Equity Targets

Minimum Standard

The % of enrolled FRNYC students who are SWD and MLL should reflect the school's overall SWD and MLL population percentages. Significant gaps trigger a school-level equity review.

Strong Practice

High-performing FRNYC schools actively over-recruit SWD and MLL students early in the cycle, knowing some will drop due to scheduling — building a buffer against gaps at final enrollment.

What Success Looks Like — KIMS Metrics

Define success before you begin. Track it weekly. Escalate when benchmarks are missed — don't wait for June.

Success in FRNYC recruitment and retention is measurable. The table below defines each outcome, how to measure it, and which system records it. Any metric more than 10 points below benchmark at the midpoint of the cycle requires a specific, documented intervention — not just increased promotion.

Outcome How to Measure It System / Timing
Pathway seats filled at enrollment KIMS enrollment numbers; % of target cohort enrolled by October 1 KIMS · Weekly Sept–Oct
Students reflect genuine pathway interest Interest survey at enrollment; student self-report in advising check-ins School-managed · May + Sept
Families understand the program Parent information session attendance; parent survey after enrollment School-managed · October
Students stay through graduation Year-over-year cohort retention rate by pathway KIMS · Reviewed each June
Students complete WBL and ECC KIMS data: WBL hours logged; ECC credits accrued by grade level KIMS · Each semester
Equitable SWD & MLL representation % of enrolled FRNYC students who are SWD and MLL vs. school population % KIMS + school data · At enrollment

A

Appendix A — Messaging by Audience

Lead with what they get — not with what FRNYC is. Every audience has a different primary motivation. Know it. Lead with it.

Messaging consistency across every touchpoint is non-negotiable. Everyone who communicates about FRNYC — from a coordinator to a front-desk staffer to a student ambassador — should follow this guide. Generic program language does not convert. Specific, audience-relevant language does.

Audience ✓ Say This ✗ Avoid This Why It Matters
Students (first contact) "You could earn a credential and a paid internship before you graduate." "Apply to our career pathway program." Concrete value leads. "Career pathway" is abstract and signals bureaucracy, not opportunity.
Students (maybe's) "You said maybe — can we show you what a typical week looks like? No commitment required." "Think about it and get back to us when you're ready." Momentum stops the moment students have to re-initiate. Remove all friction from the next step.
Parents / Caregivers "Structured, supervised, and paid — and it strengthens their college applications." "It's a great enrichment opportunity for your child." Parents need safety, structure, and financial benefit addressed first. "Enrichment" signals optional and summer-adjacent.
School Counselors "FRNYC helps your highest-need students get real credentials before graduation. Tell me the 10 students you're most worried about." "Please encourage all your students to apply." Counselors respond to specific, manageable asks. Mass appeals feel like more work, not less.
Principals "FRNYC directly improves your WBL hours, ECC credits, and college-going outcomes — all metrics you're already accountable for." "We'd love to recruit some of your students." Principals respond to institutional benefit and accountability metrics, not program enthusiasm.
At info sessions "Let's open the form right now. I'll walk you through step one together." "Go home and complete the form when you have time." Momentum is lost the moment students leave without starting. Complete step 1 in-session, always.

"The Golden Rule: Lead with what they get — not with what FRNYC is or what FRNYC does. The program's features matter less than the audience's specific motivations. Know the motivation. Lead with it."
