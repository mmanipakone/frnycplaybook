Introduction &
How to Use This Playbook
Who this is for, how to navigate it, and the key distinctions between Year 0 and Year 1+ schools.

Who This Playbook Is For

The FRNYC Recruitment & Retention Playbook is for everyone responsible for building and sustaining a FRNYC cohort in a NYC public high school. That means program coordinators, school counselors, APs of instruction, and staff at new school partners preparing to launch FRNYC for the first time.

This playbook addresses two audiences with distinct needs:

Year 0 — New FRNYC Schools
  • Setting up FRNYC from scratch
  • Designating your FRNYC coordinator
  • Building counselor buy-in before December
  • Communicating pathway codes in NYCDOE HS application
  • Planning your first open house
  • Setting baseline enrollment targets
Year 1+ — Existing FRNYC Schools
  • Reviewing last year's data and conversion rates
  • Rebuilding the ambassador corps
  • Improving the interest survey response process
  • Closing specific equity gaps (SWD, MLL)
  • Strengthening the summer gap retention touchpoint
  • Aligning pathway info design to student relevance

How to Read This

Read the full playbook once before your recruitment season begins. Then use the Quick Reference Cards (Appendix B) as your week-by-week operating guide. The Messaging Guide (Appendix A) should be shared with anyone who speaks about FRNYC — from school staff to student ambassadors.

⚡

This playbook is prescriptive, not aspirational. Every section answers: what to do, why it works, and when to do it. If a recommendation lacks those three elements, it has not earned its place in this document.

Key Terms
FRNYC NYC DOE career pathway program placing students into courses, work-based learning, and early college credit. Managed by OSP.
Recruitment The full system from first awareness to confirmed enrollment. Not a campaign — a year-round relationship operation.
Retention Keeping students enrolled through 12th grade credential completion. Recruitment without retention wastes both.
SWD / MLL Students with Disabilities and Multilingual Learners — frequently underrepresented due to structural barriers, not disinterest.
KIMS NYC DOE system tracking enrollment, WBL hours, and ECC credits. October 1 is the primary accountability deadline.
WBL / ECC Work-Based Learning (internships, shadows, simulations) and Early College Credit (college coursework before graduation).
Decision Day The formal commitment event for incoming students. A public ritual — not a form. Public commitment is more durable than private choice.

Section 02
Five Core Principles
These principles hold across every school, every grade, and every stakeholder. They explain why some FRNYC schools consistently outperform others.

Every tactic in this playbook is an application of one or more of these five principles. Before implementing anything, ask: which principle does this support, and is that the right one for this moment in the cycle?

01
Timing

Start before it feels urgent.

Recruitment is a year-round process that begins in 8th grade, bleeding into 9th. Schools that treat it as a spring campaign consistently miss the window. Every semester has a distinct recruitment goal. Missing one means starting the next from behind — and there is no catch-up month.

02
Integration

Make it school culture, not a side project.

FRNYC must live in school culture and curriculum — not in one coordinator's inbox. The Career Explorations course, advisory mentions, staff buy-in, and visible student success are all recruitment infrastructure. A coordinator recruiting alone will always underperform a school where FRNYC is woven into how the school works.

03
Social Proofing

Students follow students.

Students choose what students who look like them have chosen. Near-peer ambassadors, alumni panels, and visible student work are among the highest-converting recruitment investments available. A senior speaking authentically for 3 minutes at an advisory outperforms a brochure by a factor of ten. Build your ambassador corps before November — not in March.

04
Relevance

Lead with what they get, not what FRNYC is.

Students enroll and stay when they see how a pathway connects to their real life. Abstract language about "career readiness" does not move students. Specific language does: "You could earn your EPA certification for free — that credential normally costs $30,000." Students who chose a pathway aligned to personal meaning stay. Students who drifted into one often leave.

05
Equity

Underrepresentation is a systems problem — not a preferences problem.

Students with Disabilities and Multilingual Learners are frequently underrepresented in FRNYC pathways — not because they aren't interested, but because structural barriers (scheduling conflicts, English-only materials, prerequisite requirements) push them out before anyone notices. Strong programs actively track who is and isn't enrolled, set explicit equity targets, and problem-solve barriers before they become attrition. Equity is not a separate initiative. It is a test of whether the other four principles are actually working.

Section 03
The Recruitment Architecture
A five-stage, year-round system — not a one-time campaign. Each stage requires specific actions, not just awareness.

Effective FRNYC recruitment follows a five-stage architecture. The stages are sequential — skipping or compressing any stage reduces conversion at the next. Awareness without exploration is just information. Exploration without commitment is just curiosity. This sequence is not aspirational; it is the operating logic of every high-performing FRNYC school.

① Awareness

Students learn FRNYC exists and that it's for students like them. Channel: announcements, Career Explorations course, peer word-of-mouth, 8th grade open houses.

Risk: Curiosity is fragile. Awareness without follow-up decays within days.

② Exploration

Students have meaningful contact with what the pathway actually delivers: panels with alumni, site visits, near-peer presentations, Decision Day previews.

Goal: Identity integration — "this is for someone like me."

③ Commitment

Students make a formal decision. Decision Day is a ritual, not a form. Private choices are fragile; public commitments are durable. Application support at the moment of commitment.

Risk: Summer gap erodes commitment. Check in.

④ Experience

Students are enrolled and actively participating. Quality drives retention: relevant curriculum, strong teachers, and WBL placements that connect to stated interests.

⑤ Completion

Students earn credentials (ECC, WBL hours, certifications) before graduation. Completers are your strongest next-year recruiters. Invest in alumni conversion.

"Stickiness comes from coupling awareness with exposure in a structured sequence. At a point when interest is integrated into their sense of self, identity is more durable than interest."

Section 04
Filling the Pipeline —
8th Grade Recruitment
The pipeline that feeds 9th grade begins in 8th. Schools that wait lose two years of cultivation before realizing it.

The 9th grade pathway begins in 8th grade. Schools that wait until 10th grade to introduce pathways consistently struggle to fill cohorts with genuinely interested students. Building relationships with middle school counselors, running open houses, and activating student ambassadors before the NYCDOE application deadline are the three highest-leverage investments in the incoming pipeline.

Small-Group Touchpoints: The Highest-Converting Strategy

Bring currently enrolled FRNYC students to every fair and open house. Students who have experience in the pathway can speak to the value it will present for a prospective student and their families. Students who completed an internship, earned college credits, or went on a career trip can speak concretely about what the program delivers. Formalizing an ambassador role for these students — with a short script and clear talking points — is a low-cost, high-impact recruitment investment.

Key Moment

The open house 2–3 weeks before the NYCDOE application deadline is the highest-leverage event in 8th grade recruitment. Everything else supports it.

Signal of Interest: The NYCDOE HS Application

The NYCDOE high school application is the equivalent of a formal interest survey for 8th graders. Students who list an FRNYC-affiliated school's pathway code as their first or second choice have self-identified as interested. Schools should communicate specific pathway codes clearly at open houses and on all materials. Schools should recommend that families list both codes (first and second choice within the same school) to maximize the chance of placement.

What to Do at Open Houses

  • Show what the pathway looks like through panels, site visits, and peer testimonials
  • Have student ambassadors give 2–3 minute talks with a clear script
  • Display actual student work — projects, portfolios, credentials earned
  • Announce the pathway code and walk families through the application together
  • Collect contact info for follow-up (separate from the NYCDOE app)

After the Open House

  • Follow up within 48 hours with interested families via email or text
  • Share a brief summary of pathway outcomes (credential, wages, college credit)
  • Track which students listed your pathway code; flag for personal outreach
  • Students who said yes but can't be scheduled due to AP/IEP conflicts get an individual conversation — not a form rejection
⚡

Year 0 tip: If this is your school's first year, prioritize 2 high-quality open house events over 5 generic ones. Depth converts. Breadth just builds name recognition.

Section 05
The Internal Recruitment
Cycle — 9th Grade
The most important recruitment event of the year happens inside your school, in May, with every 9th grader. Everyone responds.

Once students arrive in 9th grade, the internal recruitment cycle takes over. This cycle has four phases — Generate Awareness, Interest Survey, Review & Reconcile, Confirm & Close — each requiring specific, coordinated action. No phase is optional.

Generate Awareness — September through Spring Break

The 9th grade year begins with the Career Explorations course. Here students are learning about the pathways and how each relates to their real life. This is a crucial point to inform students fully and honestly about the requirements of each pathway and get them aligned on their interests.

Near-peer presentations run throughout this window. Currently enrolled FRNYC seniors — including those who were not in FRNYC — visit advisories and speak authentically. Reports from pan-American staff: "UA reports 75%+ opt-in from 9th graders who took Foundations of Health and attended spring break lunch workshops."

Interest Survey — May (Critical Design)

In early May, a PowerPoint is presented in all 9th grade advisories. Within 1–2 weeks, a Google Form interest survey goes to every 9th grader.

Critical Design Choice

The survey is NOT an opt-in form. Every student must respond: Yes, I'm interested; No, I'm not interested; or Maybe, I want to know more. This ensures no one is missed and no one is assumed to be uninterested.

Review & Reconcile — With Leadership

The principal, AP of Instruction, and grade team leader review survey results together. Pathway teachers who taught the 9th grade preview course weigh in on which students they believe are ready. The team cross-references results against the master schedule: AP class conflicts, mandated services, IEP accommodations, and grade repetition situations may prevent some interested students from participating. Students who said yes but can't be scheduled receive an individual conversation.

Confirm & Close

Accepted students receive congratulations letters, FRNYC stickers, or other branded materials. An end-of-year survey captures any remaining student interest before summer, which is used for September backfill. The fall schedule for 10th grade is built around the confirmed cohort list.

⚡

The summer gap is an underused retention touchpoint. A postcard, a group chat, or a short summer check-in between June acceptance and September arrival prevents enthusiasm from fading. Do not skip this.

Section 06
Pathway Information Design
Students and families enroll and stay when pathway information is clear, concrete, and directly connected to real outcomes.

Schools should move away from generic program descriptions and adopt a consistent, student-centered format. Abstract language about "career readiness" does not move students. Specific language does: "You could earn your EPA certification for refrigerants for free, which normally costs $30,000."

Every FRNYC pathway should be able to answer four questions. If your pathway cannot answer all four clearly and specifically, students and families will not enroll — or will not stay.

The Outcome — Why It Matters

What careers does this open?

  • Clear examples of careers and associated wages
  • Emphasis on tangible benefits: income, credentials, college credits
  • Avoid abstract terms like "career readiness" or "industry exposure"
The Skills — What Students Will Learn

What will students be able to do?

  • Specific, practical skills students will develop
  • Focus on what students can do, not what teachers will cover
  • Concrete demonstrations: portfolio, certification, project
The Experience — What Students Will Actually Do

What does the day-to-day look like?

  • Examples of projects, fieldwork, and work-based learning
  • Description of real-world exposure: sites, partners, environments
  • Photos of actual student work — not stock images
The Payoff — What Students Gain

What do they leave with?

  • Certifications earned (industry-recognized)
  • College credits completed before graduation
  • Work experience and internship documentation

Example: Generic vs. Effective

✗ Generic (Don't Use) ✓ Effective (Use This)
"Students will develop career readiness skills in a healthcare pathway." "By June, you'll have 54 hours of clinical experience at a hospital partner and an EMT certification that can get you a job starting at $19/hour."
"This technology pathway prepares students for careers in computing." "You'll build a portfolio of 3 coded projects, earn a Google IT Support certificate, and be eligible for college-level CS credit at CUNY."
⚡

Students who choose pathways aligned to personal meaning stay. Students who were placed or drifted into a pathway often leave. Build structures that help students reflect on who they are and what they care about before committing.

Retention — Keeping Students
Through Completion
Filling seats at the start of the year is not the goal. Filling credentials at the end of four years is.

Students who enrolled but don't complete represent a double failure: a missed credential for the student and a wasted recruitment investment for the school. Retention is a recruitment problem — the causes of dropout are almost always visible at the enrollment stage if schools know what to look for.

🎯

Pillar 1 — Relevance

  • Students stay when pathway connects to real life and career goals
  • Structured opportunities for career exploration before commitment — panels, site visits, job shadows
  • Interest surveys that ask "why does this pathway matter to you?"
  • Pathway info uses Outcome/Skills/Experience/Payoff format — not abstract descriptions
👩‍🏫

Pillar 2 — Teacher Quality

  • Teachers who double as advisors are consequential to retention
  • A "good fit" pathway teacher reviews curriculum and adapts it for their specific students
  • Teachers hold advising-style conversations — about identity, interests, and pathway fit
  • Students in focus groups consistently cite teacher relationships as primary retention factor
🧭

Pillar 3 — Advising

  • 1-on-1 advising conversations about student identity + pathway fit are essential
  • One-on-one advising is the highest-leverage retention tool — it surfaces misalignment before dropout
  • Early warning signs: dropping advisory, missing WBL hours, declining grades in pathway course
  • Intervention: individual conversation, not a group session

The Summer Gap — An Underused Retention Touchpoint

The period between June acceptance and September arrival consistently erodes commitment. Students get summer jobs, lose track of their plans, or simply forget the excitement of Decision Day. A postcard, a group chat message, or a 5-minute phone call prevents enthusiasm from fading. This is the lowest-cost, highest-leverage retention investment available.

Early Warning Signs (Act Within 1 Week)

  • Student misses 3+ advisory sessions in a row
  • WBL hours significantly below cohort average at midpoint
  • Student expresses disinterest in pathway content directly
  • Grade in pathway course drops below 65

What Doesn't Work for Retention

  • Waiting until the end of a quarter to check in
  • Group interventions when the problem is individual misalignment
  • Assuming disengagement = disinterest in FRNYC broadly
  • Not tracking WBL hours until the final semester

Equity —
SWD & MLL Access
Underrepresentation is not a sign of disinterest. It is a sign of structural barriers that schools are responsible for removing.

Students with Disabilities (SWD) and Multilingual Learners (MLL) are frequently underrepresented in FRNYC pathways — not because they aren't interested, but because structural barriers (scheduling, language access, ECC prerequisites) push them out. Strong programs actively track who is and isn't enrolled, set explicit equity targets, and problem-solve barriers before they become attrition.

1
Scheduling Conflicts (SWD)

IEP mandated services, AP class placement, and grade repetition pull SWD students out of pathway time slots before anyone notices.

Fix

Principal + AP cross-reference survey results with master schedule before finalizing cohort. Students blocked by schedule get individual conversations and alternative slots, not form rejections.

2
Language Access (MLL)

Open house materials, interest surveys, and pathway descriptions are English-only. MLL families receive less information and make less-informed decisions.

Fix

Translate all core pathway materials (1-pager, interest survey, Decision Day communication) into the top 3 home languages in the school. Recruit bilingual near-peer ambassadors.

3
ECC Prerequisites (Both)

Some Early College Credit courses have prerequisite requirements that disproportionately exclude SWD and MLL students who are on track but not in accelerated sequences.

Fix

Audit prerequisite lists annually. Identify which barriers are structural requirements vs. inherited assumptions. Work with OSP to expand access pathways for qualified SWD/MLL students.

4
Lack of Representation in Outreach

Pathway materials, ambassador corps, and outreach presentations feature limited representation of SWD and MLL students, signaling that these pathways are "not for them."

Fix

Intentionally recruit SWD and MLL alumni as ambassadors. Review all visual materials for representation. Partner with school's ENL and Special Education departments for co-outreach.

Equity Targets

Minimum Standard

The % of enrolled FRNYC students who are SWD and MLL should reflect the school's SWD and MLL population percentages. Significant gaps trigger a school-level equity review.

Strong Practice

High-performing FRNYC schools actively over-recruit SWD and MLL students early in the cycle, knowing some will drop due to scheduling — building a buffer against gaps at enrollment.

What Success Looks Like —
KIMS Metrics
Define success before you begin. Track it weekly. Escalate when benchmarks are missed — don't wait for June.

Success in FRNYC recruitment and retention is measurable. The following table defines each outcome, how to measure it, and the system that records it. Any metric more than 10 points below benchmark by the midpoint of the cycle requires a specific, documented intervention — not just increased promotion.

Outcome How to Measure It System / Timing
Pathway seats are filled at enrollment KIMS enrollment numbers; % of target cohort enrolled by October 1 KIMS · Reviewed weekly Sept–Oct
Students reflect genuine interest in their pathway Interest survey at enrollment; student self-report in advising check-ins School-managed · May + September
Families understand the program Parent information session attendance; parent survey after enrollment School-managed · October
Students stay through graduation Year-over-year cohort retention rate by pathway KIMS · Reviewed each June
Students complete WBL and ECC requirements KIMS data: WBL hours logged; ECC credits accrued by grade level KIMS · Reviewed each semester
Equitable representation (SWD & MLL) % of enrolled students who are SWD and MLL vs. school population % KIMS + school data · Reviewed at enrollment

Appendix A
Messaging by Audience
Lead with what they get — not with what FRNYC is. Every audience has a different primary motivation. Know it. Lead with it.

Messaging consistency across every touchpoint is non-negotiable. Everyone who communicates about FRNYC — from a coordinator to a front-desk staffer to a student ambassador — should follow this guide. Generic program language does not convert. Specific, audience-relevant language does.

Audience ✓ Say This ✗ Avoid This Why It Matters
Students (first contact) "You could earn a credential and a paid internship before you graduate." "Apply to our career pathway program." Concrete value leads. "Career pathway" is abstract and signals bureaucracy, not opportunity.
Students (maybe's) "You said maybe — can we show you what a typical week looks like? No commitment required." "Think about it and get back to us when you're ready." Momentum stops the moment students have to re-initiate. Remove all friction from the next step.
Parents / Caregivers "Structured, supervised, and paid — and it strengthens their college applications." "It's a great enrichment opportunity for your child." Parents need safety, structure, and financial benefit addressed first. "Enrichment" signals optional and summer-school-adjacent.
School Counselors "FRNYC helps your highest-need students get real credentials before graduation. Tell me the 10 students you're most worried about." "Please encourage all your students to apply." Counselors respond to specific, manageable asks. Mass appeals feel like more work.
Principals "FRNYC directly improves your WBL hours, ECC credits, and college-going outcomes — all metrics you're already accountable for." "We'd love to recruit some of your students." Principals respond to institutional benefit and accountability metrics, not program enthusiasm.
At info sessions "Let's open the form right now. I'll walk you through step one together." "Go home and complete the form when you have time." Momentum is lost the moment students leave without starting. Complete step 1 in-session.

"The Golden Rule: Lead with what they get — not with what FRNYC is or what FRNYC does. The program's features matter less than the audience's specific motivations. Know the motivation. Lead with it."
