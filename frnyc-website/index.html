Section 02

5 Core Principles
of Strong Recruitment

Across schools, strong recruitment systems share consistent characteristics. These principles don't operate separately — they drive movement through every stage of the framework.

01
Awareness → Commitment

Timing

Recruitment is a year-round process that begins before students enter high school and continues through 9th grade. Schools that delay until 10th grade struggle.

  • Begin in 8th grade — external awareness
  • Build through 9th grade — exposure and decision-making
02
Exposure → Experience

Integration

Programs that operate as separate add-ons struggle with both recruitment and retention. Embedded programs see stronger enrollment and persistence.

  • Embedded in advisory and courses
  • Part of school culture, not a separate silo
  • Principal actively involved in messaging
03
Awareness → Exposure

Social Proof

Students are most influenced by other students. Peer-to-peer communication builds trust, makes pathways feel real, and increases interest and enrollment.

  • Builds authentic trust
  • Makes pathways feel concrete and achievable
  • Increases interest more than any flyer
04
Exposure → Commitment

Relevance

Students enroll and stay when they understand what they will do, what they will gain, and how it connects to their future.

  • Lead with concrete outcomes, not jargon
  • "Career readiness" moves no one
  • Paid internships and certifications move everyone
05
All Stages

Equity

Students with disabilities and multilingual learners are underrepresented due to structural barriers — not lack of interest. Strong programs proactively address this.

  • Track representation at every stage
  • Identify specific barriers — don't assume
  • Address barriers before they become attrition
Section 03A

Student Recruitment
Journey

Recruitment is not a single moment. It is a continuous process that begins before enrollment and continues through graduation. Each grade represents a different stage.

8th Grade
Awareness & Initial Exposure

Before High School

Introduce the pathway to students and families. Build enough understanding for families to make informed high school choices.

  • High school fairs, borough events, open houses
  • Schedule open houses close to application deadlines
  • Use current students as ambassadors
  • Concrete examples land better than abstract descriptions
8
9
9th Grade
Exposure & Decision-Making

The Most Important Year

Students are introduced through Career Explorations, advisory, and school-wide messaging. Then given real experiences before the decision point.

  • Course previews, classroom visits, guest speakers
  • Near-peer presentations from current students
  • Interest survey: every student responds yes / no / maybe
  • School team reviews, identifies barriers (IEP, ENL, conflicts)
  • Acceptance communication + cohort introduction
10th Grade
Experience Begins

Early Experiences Shape Retention

Students begin the pathway. The first 6 weeks are critical. Work-based learning and coursework must feel meaningful from the start.

  • Scheduling decisions — especially ECC — strongly influence retention
  • Deliver 1 strong project + 1 real-world exposure in first month
  • Review data regularly — intervene early when students disengage
  • Attendance below 90% triggers intervention within 1 week
10
11
11th Grade
Retention & Deep Engagement

Highest-Risk Year for Attrition

Workload increases, competing priorities emerge, expectations become more demanding. Strong programs re-communicate value and ensure access to meaningful WBL.

  • Re-sell the payoff twice per year: September + January
  • Show earnings potential, college credit progress, alumni examples
  • Build WBL/ECC into school day where possible
  • Multiple staff share responsibility — team-based support
12th Grade
Completion & Transition

Celebrate, Reflect, Recruit

Students complete the pathway and transition to postsecondary plans. Seniors become the most effective recruiters for the next cohort.

  • Recognize student accomplishments explicitly
  • Help students articulate what they have completed
  • Seniors provide authentic examples for 8th and 9th graders
  • Use student experiences to power future recruitment cycles

Section 03B

Staff: Whole School Model

"Programs struggle when responsibility sits with one person, communication is inconsistent, and systems are not shared."

Key Structural Insight
Whole School Integration

Strong Programs Are Part of the School

Not separate initiatives. When FRNYC is embedded in advisory, teacher communication, and school leadership structures — enrollment and retention improve.

🏫

Integration into School Culture

FRNYC is woven into advisory periods, teacher communication, and school leadership structures — not bolted on.

👤

Principal Actively Involved

The principal participates in messaging, planning, and decision-making. Visible leadership signals school-wide priority.

📋

Designated Program Owner

A single coordinator owns execution — but responsibility for recruitment and retention is shared across the full staff team.

📊

Regular Team Meetings

Biweekly review of enrollment, retention, and risks. Data is shared — not siloed. Standing meeting keeps everyone aligned.

Section 03C

Family Engagement

Section 03C

Families as Decision-Makers and Partners

Family engagement is essential — and operates differently than student recruitment. Families are key decision-makers in 8th grade and ongoing retention partners through graduation.

📢

Awareness

Families need clear explanation of what students will do, clear outcomes, and reassurance about workload.

  • Materials in appropriate languages
  • Direct from trusted school staff
  • Timed close to actual decision points
🤝

Families as Partners

Families can actively support the pathway when they are informed and treated as genuine partners — not just recipients of information.

  • Support student decision-making at home
  • Contribute to programming and events
  • Reinforce student persistence through challenges
🎉

Celebrate & Retain

Recognize milestones with families. When families see concrete progress — certifications, college credits, paid internships — they reinforce persistence.

  • Family-facing milestone communications
  • After any event: follow up within 72 hours
  • Lead every message with: "What does my child gain?"

A complete, ready-to-use guide for schools to build and sustain a student ambassador / peer mentoring model.

Includes:

  • Student ambassador role description + selection criteria
  • Training materials and talking points for presentations
  • Structures for peer-to-peer engagement

4-Step Deployment

  • Step 1: Identify ambassadors — diverse experiences, not just top performers
  • Step 2: Train on projects, WBL, ECC — including what was challenging
  • Step 3: Deploy at open houses, advisory presentations, family sessions
  • Step 4: Structure it — specific events, clear expectations, staff support

Cohort Toolkit

A structured model and set of tools to help schools build and sustain a strong student cohort experience from entry through graduation.

Includes:

  • Advisory routines and cohort-building activities
  • Peer engagement structures and milestone celebrations
  • Systems to maintain connection across years

Key Milestones to Celebrate

  • First WBL experience
  • First college credits earned
  • Certifications achieved
  • Internship placements confirmed

Recommended Implementation Sequence

Schools should not attempt to implement all tools at once. Start with the Visual 4-Year Roadmap for planning and clarity. Layer in the Ambassador Toolkit to strengthen recruitment. Build the Cohort Toolkit as a retention system over time.

Section 06

Data & Accountability

What to Track and Why

Strong Systems Track a Small Number of Key Metrics

Data is used to identify gaps, inform decisions, and support early intervention — not to produce reports. Track these four things consistently.

🎯

Interest

Survey completion rate, yes/maybe/no breakdown, by grade and student population

📝

Enrollment

Actual cohort size vs. target. Demographic breakdown vs. school demographics.

📈

Retention

Month-over-month persistence by grade. Flag when attendance drops below 90%.

⚖️

Representation

SWD and MLL participation rate. Compare cohort demographics to school demographics.

Section 07

Equity Recruitment Practices

Structural Barriers, Not Lack of Interest

Every Door Must Open Equally

Students with disabilities and multilingual learners are systematically underrepresented in FRNYC pathways. This is not because these students are less capable or interested. It is because structural barriers push them out.

What Strong Programs Do

Equity is not an add-on — it runs through every stage of the framework. Strong programs proactively audit, identify, and fix structural barriers.

  • Track SWD and MLL representation at every stage
  • Compare cohort demographics to school demographics
  • Set explicit enrollment targets for priority populations
  • Assign a staff member to resolve each identified gap
  • Ensure materials are translated and culturally responsive
  • Monitor persistence, not just initial enrollment

Common Structural Barriers

🗓️

Scheduling Conflicts

IEP services, ENL classes, and period conflicts prevent students from accessing pathway courses — even when they want to.

🌐

Language Access

Recruitment materials in English only exclude families who need information in their home language to make informed decisions.

📚

Prerequisite Assumptions

Unofficial academic gatekeeping — messaging or advising that signals the pathway is "not for them" — creates inequity without any formal policy.

💬

Representation in Messaging

When examples and ambassadors don't reflect diverse student populations, it signals to some students that the pathway isn't for people like them.

Section 08

Challenges & Solutions

A Diagnostic and Response System

When Recruitment Is Off Track

🔍

Ask the Right Question First

Don't ask: "What activity are we missing?" — Ask: "At what stage of the recruitment framework is the system breaking?"

1

Low 10th Grade Enrollment — Did Not Hit 25 Students

▼
Root Causes
  • Students did not experience the pathway
  • Exposure happened too late (April/May only)
  • Messaging was generic, not concrete
  • No 1:1 follow-up with "maybe" students
  • Survey was optional or incomplete
What to Do
  • Run 2 additional exposure sessions within 2 weeks
  • Every "maybe" student gets a 1:1 conversation within 5 days
  • Re-administer survey if completion is below 85%
  • Deploy 3–5 current students to talk to "maybe" students

Do NOT: Add more flyers, send more emails, or wait until next year.

2

Students Cannot Be Scheduled into the Pathway

▼
Root Cause
  • Recruitment and scheduling are disconnected
  • IEP/ENL service conflicts
  • Section capacity issues
What to Do
  • Cohort must be finalized BEFORE master schedule is locked
  • Add sections if demand exists — reallocate teacher load
  • Solve SWD/MLL access individually: adjust period placement
  • If unresolved: escalate to AP within 5 days

Do NOT: Assume "they can't fit." Do not drop students quietly.

3

10th Grade Attrition — Early Drop-Off

▼
Root Causes
  • Students did not choose pathway intentionally
  • First experience is weak or unclear
  • No early wins in first 6 weeks
  • ECC/WBL feels disconnected from student goals
What to Do
  • Deliver 1 strong project + 1 real-world exposure in first month
  • Run advisory session: "Why did you choose this?"
  • If attendance below 90% or failing a class → intervene within 1 week
4

11th Grade Drop-Off — Highest Risk Point

▼
Root Causes
  • Competing priorities increase — jobs, other classes
  • WBL is not meaningful or paid
  • ECC too difficult or inconvenient
  • Students don't see long-term payoff
What to Do
  • Re-sell the payoff twice per year: September + January
  • Show earnings potential, college credits, alumni examples
  • Build WBL/ECC into school day — reduce schedule conflicts
  • Show short-term job pay vs. pathway outcome comparison
5

SWD and MLL Students Are Underrepresented

▼
Root Cause
  • Structural barriers, not lack of interest
  • Schedule conflicts with services
  • Language access gaps in materials
  • Prerequisite assumptions
What to Do
  • Compare applicants vs. school population during cohort build
  • Identify specific barrier type for each excluded student
  • Assign each gap to a staff member — resolve within 1 week
  • Translate materials and diversify ambassador representation
6

Families Are Not Engaged

▼
Root Causes
  • Communication channel mismatch
  • Messaging too abstract
  • No follow-up after events
What to Do
  • Switch channels: Text, WhatsApp, direct calls — not email alone
  • Lead every message with: "What does my child gain?"
  • Contact family within 72 hours after any event
7

Staff Are Not Aligned

▼
Root Cause
  • No system — only individuals
  • Teachers say different things to students
  • Recruitment depends on one person
What to Do
  • Assign clear roles: Principal → accountability, Coordinator → execution, Counselors → advising
  • Provide a script / talking points for all staff
  • Create standing biweekly meeting to review enrollment, retention, risks

Final Diagnostic: 5 Questions

If recruitment is off track, answer these five questions. Every "no" is your problem.

Did every student experience the pathway?

Did every student complete the interest survey?

Did we follow up 1:1 with every "maybe" student?

Did we resolve all scheduling conflicts before the master schedule was locked?

Did we build cohort identity and belonging before asking for commitment?

Messaging Guide &
Quick Reference Cards

What Students Actually Respond To

Based on focus groups with current FRNYC students. The pattern: concrete, specific, and connected to real life.

Message That Landed Why It Works
"You could earn your OSHA-30 and EPA certifications for free here. Outside, those cost hundreds of dollars." Concrete dollar value; makes the abstract tangible
"In 11th grade, you'll have a paid internship. You earn money while still in high school." Paid internship is the single most cited motivator across all student groups
"You're not just taking classes, you're earning college credits that count at real colleges." College credits feel like "getting ahead"; reduces fear of falling behind
"This is not like regular school. You pick this because you want it, not because you have to." Agency and choice are central to student identity in these pathways
"The teachers here treat you like an adult, not a kid to manage." Respect and dignity resonate deeply, especially with older students
"An HVAC technician paid $30,000 for training you're getting for free right now." Real-world cost comparisons hit hard with families and students thinking about money
"By senior year, you'll have done real work, earned money, and have credits before graduation." The full picture of completion is motivating when students can see the endpoint

What NOT to Lead With

These messages are commonly used but consistently fail to move students or families.

Avoid Leading With... Replace With...
"This will make you career-ready." "By graduation, you'll have a certification, work experience, and college credits — not just a diploma."
"Future Ready is a great opportunity." "Future Ready students here got paid internships starting in 11th grade — here's what that looked like."
"This pathway aligns with industry demand." "HVAC technicians in New York City earn $80,000+ per year. You can start that path here, for free."
"You'll develop transferable skills." "You'll learn how to wire a circuit, read a blueprint, manage a budget, and interview for a job."
"We want you to be college and career ready." "You'll leave with options: go to college with credits already earned, start an apprenticeship, or go straight to work."

Card 1: The 5-Step Recruitment Process

StepWhat It Means
1. AdvertiseMake the program visible everywhere — posters, Instagram, announcements, advisory. If students don't know it exists, they can't join.
2. InformHost student AND family info sessions. Make outcomes concrete and specific. Jargon kills interest.
3. ApplyOpen a 3–4 week application window. Use a simple Google Form. Follow up personally with students who expressed interest but haven't applied.
4. IntegrateSend acceptance letters. Host a welcome event. Make enrollment feel like an accomplishment worth keeping.
5. RetainRecognize students consistently: semester milestones, internship completions, graduation cords, award ceremonies. Celebration is a retention strategy.

Warning SignRecommended Action

Declining CCI attendanceTeacher flags to coordinator. Advisor reaches out within 48 hours.
"I'm not sure this is for me"Do not dismiss. Schedule a full advising session before any paperwork.
